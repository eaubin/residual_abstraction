# Residual streams as abstract domains: an exact, small-scale experiment

## Context for readers without the originating conversation

This codebase was written at the end of a design conversation. If you are a
human or an LLM reading it cold, this section reconstructs the ideas it
implements. The code is intended to be an *informative experiment*, not a
library: every module's header comments restate the piece of the framework it
realizes, and the analysis scripts print interpretation alongside numbers.

### The question

Given a language model, what is the relationship between

- the **space of residuals**: the residual-stream activation `r_t` at
  position `t`, i.e. the model's internal summary of the prefix so far, and
- the **space of completions**: the probability measure over all future
  token sequences that the model (or the data-generating process) assigns
  after that prefix?

We want to *characterize* this relationship formally and *discover* it
empirically, in the spirit of **abstract interpretation** (Cousot & Cousot):
treat the residual as an abstract value, the completion measure as the
concrete semantics, and ask how good the abstraction is.

### Why the classical theory has to be bent before it applies

1. **No Galois connection.** Classical abstract interpretation relates two
   posets by an adjunction (alpha, gamma) and asks for *soundness*
   (the abstraction over-approximates) and *completeness*
   (`alpha . F = F# . alpha`, no precision lost). Here the concretization of a
   residual is not a *set* of completions but a *probability kernel*
   `gamma : R -> Delta(Completions)`: text generation is stochastic. Powerset
   adjunctions are the wrong category; the honest replacements are linear /
   measure-theoretic (cf. Di Pierro & Wiklicky, probabilistic abstract
   interpretation), and "completeness" becomes a **real-valued quantity** —
   a divergence such as KL between the completion distribution predicted from
   the abstraction and the true one — rather than a property one has or lacks.

2. **Sufficiency, not bisimulation.** In the static setup (prefix -> residual,
   prefix -> completion measure) there is no transition relation, so the right
   equivalence is *kernel sufficiency*: `r ~ r'` iff they induce (nearly) the
   same completion measure. An abstraction is "complete" exactly when it is a
   **sufficient statistic** for the completion kernel. Bisimulation re-enters
   only if you additionally demand coherence under autoregressive extension
   (the abstraction after one generated token should be predictable from the
   abstraction now); we note this but do not test it here. The *learning*
   trick is borrowed from bisimulation-metric RL anyway (Ferns–Panangaden–
   Precup 2004; Zhang et al. DBC, ICLR 2021; Castro et al. MICo, NeurIPS
   2021): train/select representations whose geometry matches a behavioral
   divergence.

3. **Resource-bounded completeness = V-information.** Exact concretization
   would mean running the rest of the network; in practice we ask what a
   *bounded* family of readouts (here: affine probes / low-rank projections +
   softmax heads) can extract. This is V-information (Xu et al. 2020,
   "A Theory of Usable Information under Computational Constraints"):
   information relative to an extractor class. The experiment therefore
   reports completeness *as a function of abstraction capacity k*.

### Why these toy processes

Shai et al. 2024 ("Transformers represent belief state geometry in their
residual stream") showed that for data generated by a hidden Markov process,
a trained transformer's residual stream **linearly embeds the belief state**
— the Bayesian posterior over hidden states given the prefix. The belief
state is the *minimal sufficient statistic for the entire future*, i.e. the
ideal abstract domain for the completion measure. For some processes (Mess3)
the reachable beliefs form a fractal inside the probability simplex, and that
fractal is visible in the residual stream under a learned linear projection.

We use processes where everything is **exact and closed-form**:

- **Z1R** ("zero–one–random"): emits 0, 1, then a fair coin, repeating.
  3 hidden states, vocabulary {0,1}. Trivially small; the debugging process.
- **Mess3** (Marzen & Crutchfield): 3 hidden states, 3 symbols, whose belief
  dynamics generate a fractal in the 2-simplex. The real test.

Because the hidden process is known, the *true* belief state `b_t` and the
*true* m-step completion distribution `P(next m tokens | prefix)` are
computable exactly for every prefix. Ground truth is not estimated by
sampling — abstraction error is measured, not guessed. This is what makes a
laptop-scale experiment informative.

### The experiment, stage by stage

1. **`train.py`** trains a small transformer (1–2 layers, d_model≈64) on
   sequences sampled from the process, then caches, for held-out sequences:
   the residual stream at every position, the exact belief state at that
   position, and the exact m-gram completion distribution.
2. **`analysis.py`** (calibration / replication):
   a. Affine regression residual -> belief state. High R² and, for Mess3, a
      visible fractal under the learned projection replicate Shai et al. and
      certify the pipeline.
   b. **Sufficiency curves**: for each abstraction rank k, project residuals
      to their top-k PCA subspace, fit a softmax head to the m-gram
      distribution, and report held-out mean KL(true || predicted). This is
      the quantified-completeness curve. Expected signature: an elbow at
      k = 2, the intrinsic dimension of the 3-state belief simplex —
      i.e. the empirically discovered abstraction coincides with the known
      minimal sufficient statistic.
3. **`refine.py`** (the outer loop — discovery, rejection, refinement):
   a CEGAR-style loop (counterexample-guided abstraction refinement;
   conceptually the empirical analogue of the **complete shell** of
   Giacobazzi, Ranzato & Scozzari, JACM 2000 — the coarsest refinement of an
   abstract domain that makes it complete for a given semantics):
   - *Propose* a rank-k abstraction.
   - *Test* its completeness (held-out KL).
   - *Mine incompleteness counterexamples*: prefix pairs the abstraction
     conflates (nearest neighbors in abstract space) whose true completion
     distributions diverge -> the abstraction is too coarse -> REFINE (k+1).
   - *Mine junk precision*: abstract dimensions whose removal does not hurt
     completeness -> the abstraction is needlessly fine -> COARSEN.
   - Stop at a fixed point: the empirical complete shell. On these processes
     the loop should terminate at k = 2 and the recovered 2-D geometry should
     be an affine image of the belief simplex (checked by regression).

### What this does and does not license (scope & honesty)

- The method discovers the **network's** sufficient statistics for
  completions *on the training distribution*. Concepts that barely move the
  completion measure on-distribution are invisible regardless of how real
  they are; for safety-relevant properties that manifest off-distribution
  this is a structural blind spot, not an implementation defect.
- Passing the tests certifies "functions as a sufficient summary under these
  probes and this distribution", not identity with any human ontology.
- On real LLMs no finite-capacity abstraction reaches KL≈0; here k=2 should,
  because the generating process is genuinely 3-state. That contrast is the
  point of the calibration.

## Running it (macOS)

```bash
python3 -m venv .venv && source .venv/bin/activate
pip install -r requirements.txt        # numpy, torch, matplotlib

python3 run_all.py --process z1r       # ~2–5 min on CPU; pipeline check
python3 run_all.py --process mess3     # ~10–25 min on CPU/MPS; the real one
```

Outputs land in `out/<process>/`:
- `cache.npz` — residuals + exact beliefs + exact m-gram distributions
- `belief_regression.png` — true vs residual-decoded belief geometry
  (for Mess3: the fractal)
- `sufficiency_curve.png` — KL vs abstraction rank k (the completeness curve)
- `refine_log.txt` — narrated CEGAR trajectory and its fixed point

Apple Silicon: training auto-selects `mps` when available; everything also
runs on plain CPU. `analysis.py` and `refine.py` are numpy-only and operate
on the cache, so they can be rerun cheaply without torch in the environment.
